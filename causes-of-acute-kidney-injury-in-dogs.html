Introduction to Acute Kidney Injury in Dogs

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a sudden, often dramatic, loss of kidney function that develops over hours to days. Unlike chronic kidney disease, which progresses slowly over months or years, AKI typically appears quickly and can be life-threatening if not recognised and treated promptly.

The kidneys’ primary role is to filter waste products and excess fluid from the blood, regulate electrolytes, and help control blood pressure. When an acute injury occurs, these jobs are disrupted, leading to a rapid buildup of toxins, fluid imbalance, and potentially severe illness. Some dogs can fully or partially recover from AKI, while others may be left with permanent kidney damage or progress to chronic kidney disease.

Because AKI can look similar to other illnesses (vomiting, lethargy, poor appetite), blood and urine tests are essential to confirm the diagnosis and guide treatment.

Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

AKI can arise from many different triggers. Vets often group the causes into three broad categories: pre-renal (before the kidney), renal (direct kidney damage), and post-renal (after the kidney, affecting urine outflow).

Pre-Renal Causes (Reduced Blood Flow to the Kidneys)

  • Severe dehydration from vomiting, diarrhoea, heatstroke, or inadequate water intake
  • Low blood pressure due to shock, blood loss, or severe infection (sepsis)
  • Heart disease that reduces circulation to the kidneys

Renal Causes (Direct Damage to Kidney Tissue)

  • Toxins: e.g., antifreeze (ethylene glycol), certain lilies (in cats, occasionally in dogs), grapes and raisins, some human medications (such as ibuprofen or naproxen), household chemicals, and some rodent poisons
  • Infectious diseases: such as leptospirosis or severe systemic infections that damage the kidneys
  • Ischaemic injury: prolonged poor blood flow to the kidneys, such as during major surgery or severe shock
  • Drug side effects: certain antibiotics, NSAIDs, chemotherapy drugs, and contrast agents can be nephrotoxic in some situations
  • Immune-mediated injury: conditions where the body’s immune system attacks kidney tissue or the filtering units (glomeruli)

Post-Renal Causes (Problems After Urine Leaves the Kidneys)

  • Urinary obstruction, such as bladder stones, urethral stones, or blood clots blocking urine flow
  • Ruptured bladder or ureter, causing urine to leak into the abdomen
  • Severe prostate enlargement or tumours compressing the urinary tract

In many real-life cases, more than one factor is involved. For example, a dog may be mildly dehydrated from vomiting and also receive a medication that is hard on the kidneys, tipping them into AKI.

Risk Factors for Developing AKI

Any dog can develop acute kidney injury, but some are at higher risk due to age, lifestyle, or underlying health problems.

Common Risk Factors

  • Severe dehydration or fluid loss from gastrointestinal disease, heatstroke, or inadequate water availability
  • Exposure to toxins such as antifreeze, grapes/raisins, certain plants, or inappropriate human medications
  • Pre-existing kidney disease (chronic kidney disease makes the kidneys more vulnerable to further injury)
  • Recent anaesthesia or major surgery, especially in unstable patients or those with low blood pressure
  • Use of nephrotoxic drugs (drugs that can damage kidneys) without careful monitoring
  • Severe infections, including leptospirosis or systemic infections that affect blood pressure and organ function
  • Urinary tract obstruction from stones, tumours, or severe inflammation

Puppies and senior dogs may be more vulnerable due to immature or already-compromised kidneys and a reduced ability to compensate for rapid changes in hydration or blood pressure.

Clinical Signs and Symptoms

Signs of acute kidney injury can appear suddenly and may worsen quickly. Some are subtle at first, while others are dramatic and clearly abnormal.

Early and Common Signs

  • Reduced or absent appetite
  • Lethargy or unusual tiredness
  • Vomiting and/or diarrhoea
  • Drinking more or less than usual
  • Changes in urination – producing very little urine, or sometimes urinating more frequently early on

More Severe Signs

  • Bad breath, sometimes with a “urine-like” or metallic smell
  • Ulcers in the mouth, drooling, or pawing at the mouth
  • Weakness, collapse, or disorientation
  • Swollen limbs or fluid in the abdomen (oedema)
  • Seizures or tremors in advanced or complicated cases

These signs are not specific to AKI and can overlap with other serious illnesses, so prompt veterinary assessment is essential whenever you see them.

Diagnosis and Treatment Options

Diagnosing acute kidney injury involves combining clinical findings with laboratory tests and imaging. Early diagnosis gives the best chance of stabilising your dog and limiting long-term damage.

How AKI Is Diagnosed

  • History and physical exam: including toxin exposure, recent trauma, medications, surgery, or illness
  • Blood tests: measuring urea/BUN, creatinine, phosphorus, electrolytes, and sometimes SDMA and acid–base status
  • Urinalysis: checking urine concentration, protein levels, sediment, and evidence of infection or crystals
  • Imaging: abdominal ultrasound or X-rays to assess kidney size, structure, and look for obstructions or stones
  • Blood pressure measurement: since high or low blood pressure can be both a cause and consequence of AKI

Treatment Approaches

Treatment focuses on stabilising your dog, correcting fluid and electrolyte imbalances, and addressing the underlying cause:

  • Intravenous fluids to restore hydration, support blood pressure, and help flush toxins
  • Managing electrolytes (such as potassium and sodium) and acid–base balance
  • Addressing the trigger: antidotes for certain toxins, antibiotics for infections, surgery or catheterisation for urinary obstruction, or stopping nephrotoxic drugs
  • Medications for nausea, vomiting, and stomach protection to improve comfort and appetite
  • Pain relief where appropriate, using kidney-safe options
  • Nutritional support: renal-friendly diet, feeding tubes in some cases, or temporary assisted feeding
  • Advanced therapies: dialysis or haemofiltration in specialised centres for severe or non-responsive cases

Many dogs with AKI need hospitalisation and intensive monitoring, especially in the first few days. The exact plan depends on the cause, how early the problem is caught, and your dog’s overall health.

Prevention Strategies

While not all cases of acute kidney injury can be prevented, there are practical steps you can take to reduce your dog’s risk.

Everyday Prevention Tips

  • Keep antifreeze, cleaning products, rodent poisons, and medications securely out of reach.
  • Never give human painkillers (like ibuprofen or naproxen) unless specifically prescribed by a vet.
  • Avoid allowing your dog to eat grapes, raisins, or unknown plants.
  • Ensure constant access to fresh water, especially in hot weather or after exercise.
  • Seek prompt veterinary care for vomiting, diarrhoea, heatstroke, or severe lethargy to prevent dehydration.
  • Follow your vet’s advice about blood tests and monitoring if your dog is on long-term medications that can affect the kidneys.
  • Keep up to date with vaccination and parasite control, including protection against diseases linked to kidney damage in your area (for example, leptospirosis where relevant).

Early recognition of illness, rapid rehydration, and avoiding unnecessary toxin exposure are the most effective ways to reduce the likelihood and severity of AKI.

FAQs

Q: What are the common causes of acute kidney injury in dogs?

A: Common causes include severe dehydration or shock, toxic exposures (such as antifreeze, grapes/raisins, some human medications, or certain plants), serious infections like leptospirosis, side effects of certain drugs, reduced blood flow to the kidneys during major illness or surgery, and urinary blockages caused by stones or tumours.

Q: How can I tell if my dog has acute kidney injury?

A: Dogs with AKI often show sudden changes such as vomiting, loss of appetite, lethargy, and changes in drinking or urination (producing very little or no urine, or urinating much more at first). Bad breath, mouth ulcers, weakness, or collapse can occur in more advanced cases. These signs are not specific, so blood and urine tests at the vet are needed to confirm AKI.

Q: What breeds are more prone to acute kidney injury?

A: Any breed can develop AKI, as it is usually triggered by toxins, infections, dehydration, or other external factors. Some breeds may be over-represented in certain causes (for example, breeds prone to urinary stones or hereditary kidney conditions), but AKI is not limited to specific breeds in the way some genetic diseases are.

Q: Are there specific toxins that can cause kidney injury in dogs?

A: Yes. Important examples include antifreeze (ethylene glycol), grapes and raisins, some human painkillers (NSAIDs such as ibuprofen or naproxen), certain rodent poisons, some plants, and in some cases heavy metals or other industrial chemicals. If you suspect your dog has eaten or licked something toxic, contact your vet or a poison helpline immediately.

Q: How is acute kidney injury diagnosed in dogs?

A: Diagnosis is based on a combination of history, physical examination, blood tests (looking at creatinine, urea/BUN, electrolytes, and other markers), urinalysis, and often imaging such as ultrasound or X-rays. These tests help confirm that the kidneys are not working properly and may also reveal the underlying cause, such as stones or infection.

Q: What treatment options are available for dogs with AKI?

A: Treatment usually includes intravenous fluids, careful monitoring of electrolytes and urine output, medications for nausea and pain, and specific therapies for the cause (for example, antidotes or drug withdrawal for toxin exposure, antibiotics for infection, or surgery to relieve urinary obstruction). In severe cases, dialysis or advanced supportive care in a referral centre may be recommended.

Q: Can acute kidney injury in dogs be prevented?

A: Not every case can be prevented, but the risk can be reduced by keeping toxins and medications out of reach, never giving human drugs without veterinary advice, maintaining good hydration, seeking early treatment for vomiting or diarrhoea, and following your vet’s monitoring plan if your dog is on long-term medications or has existing health problems.

Q: How does dehydration affect kidney function in dogs?

A: Dehydration reduces blood flow to the kidneys and concentrates toxins in the bloodstream. If severe or prolonged, this reduced circulation can damage kidney tissue and trigger acute kidney injury. That’s why rapid treatment of dehydration with appropriate fluids is so important in sick or heat-stressed dogs.

Q: What is the prognosis for dogs with acute kidney injury?

A: The outlook varies widely. Some dogs, especially those diagnosed early and treated aggressively, can recover most or all of their kidney function. Others may be left with chronic kidney disease or, in severe cases, may not survive despite intensive care. Prognosis depends on the cause, how quickly treatment begins, the dog’s response to therapy, and whether other organs are also affected.
