Understanding Kidney Failure in Dogs

“Kidney failure” in dogs is a general term that means the kidneys are no longer able to perform their normal jobs: filtering waste products, balancing fluids and electrolytes, and helping regulate blood pressure and red blood cell production. Vets usually distinguish between acute kidney injury (AKI), which appears suddenly over hours to days, and chronic kidney disease (CKD), which develops gradually over months or years.

In both forms, waste products and excess fluids build up in the body. This can lead to nausea, vomiting, dehydration, weight loss, and changes in urination. Treatment aims to support the kidneys, remove toxins, correct imbalances, and treat the underlying cause whenever possible.

While most kidney damage cannot be fully reversed, many dogs can be stabilised and enjoy months or years of good-quality life with thoughtful treatment and monitoring.

Symptoms of Kidney Failure

Signs of kidney failure can range from subtle to dramatic. Early recognition and prompt veterinary care often make a big difference to outcome.

Common Symptoms

  • Increased thirst and urination, or in late stages, very little urine production
  • Reduced appetite or becoming very picky with food
  • Weight loss and muscle wasting
  • Lethargy, weakness, or reduced interest in walks and play
  • Bad breath (sometimes with a “metallic” or urine-like smell)
  • Vomiting, nausea, lip-licking, or drooling

More Advanced or Emergency Signs

  • Collapse or severe weakness
  • Ulcers in the mouth or pale gums
  • Seizures, twitching, or confusion
  • Swollen limbs or fluid in the belly

These signs can overlap with other serious illnesses. Blood and urine tests are needed to confirm kidney failure and understand how advanced it is.

Diagnosis and Testing

Vets diagnose kidney failure by combining your dog’s history and symptoms with laboratory tests and sometimes imaging. This helps determine whether the problem is acute, chronic, or a mix of both, and guides treatment decisions.

Key Diagnostic Steps

  • History and physical examination: including questions about water intake, urination, diet, medications, possible toxin exposure, and any recent illness or surgery.
  • Blood tests: to measure creatinine, urea/BUN, SDMA, phosphorus, electrolytes (such as potassium and sodium), and sometimes red blood cell counts and acid–base balance.
  • Urinalysis: to check urine concentration, look for protein, blood, crystals, or infection, and detect changes in how well the kidneys are concentrating urine.
  • Imaging: ultrasound or X-rays to assess kidney size and structure and to look for stones, cysts, or obstructions.
  • Blood pressure measurement: as high blood pressure is common in dogs with kidney disease and can worsen damage.

Results from these tests are often used with established guidelines (such as IRIS staging) to classify the severity of disease and choose the most appropriate treatments.

Treatment Options

Treatment for kidney failure is tailored to the type (acute vs chronic), severity, and underlying cause. In many cases, several approaches are used together.

Hospital-Based Treatments

  • Intravenous fluids: to correct dehydration, improve circulation to the kidneys, and help flush toxins.
  • Electrolyte and acid–base correction: adjusting potassium, sodium, and acid–base levels to protect the heart, muscles, and brain.
  • Management of the underlying cause: antidotes or decontamination for known toxins, antibiotics for infections such as leptospirosis, surgery or catheterisation to relieve urinary blockages, and stopping or modifying potentially kidney-harming drugs.
  • Nausea and vomiting control: anti-nausea medications, antacids, and stomach protectants to improve comfort and appetite.
  • Pain management: using kidney-safe pain relief where needed (for example, with stones or inflammation).
  • Advanced therapies: in some specialist centres, dialysis or haemofiltration may be available for severe or non-responsive cases of acute kidney injury.

Home and Long-Term Management

  • Renal-support diet: low-phosphorus, moderate-protein, controlled-sodium diets designed specifically for kidney disease.
  • Blood pressure control: medications to treat high blood pressure and protect the kidneys and other organs.
  • Proteinuria (urine protein) control: drugs such as ACE inhibitors or other agents may be used when there is significant protein loss in the urine.
  • Phosphate binders: medications that bind phosphorus in the gut when blood phosphorus stays high despite a renal diet.
  • Anti-nausea and appetite support: ongoing use of antiemetics and appetite stimulants if needed, to keep your dog eating comfortably.
  • Subcutaneous fluids: in some dogs with chronic kidney disease, fluids given under the skin at home help maintain hydration.

Every dog is different. Your vet will explain which treatments are realistic and appropriate for your dog’s stage of disease, overall health, and your home situation.

Dietary Management

Diet is one of the most powerful tools for supporting dogs with kidney failure. A well-chosen diet can reduce the kidney’s workload, help control phosphorus and toxin levels, and support a healthy weight.

Key Features of a Kidney-Friendly Diet

  • Controlled protein: moderate amounts of high-quality protein to maintain muscle without overloading the kidneys.
  • Low phosphorus: helps slow progression of kidney damage and reduce secondary complications.
  • Controlled sodium: supports blood pressure control and reduces fluid retention.
  • Higher moisture content: wet food or adding water/broth helps with hydration and palatability.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: often added from fish or algae oil to support kidney blood flow and reduce inflammation.
  • Balanced vitamins and minerals: adjusted to account for how kidney disease changes the body’s needs.

Most dogs with kidney failure benefit from a veterinary prescription renal diet. For very fussy eaters, a combination of prescription food and carefully designed home-cooked meals may be used under veterinary and nutritionist guidance. Sudden diet changes are best avoided; transition should be gradual to reduce stomach upset and improve acceptance.

Monitoring and Supportive Care

Effective kidney failure treatment is not a one-off event but an ongoing process. Regular monitoring allows your vet to adjust medications and diet to keep your dog as comfortable and stable as possible.

What Vets Monitor

  • Blood tests: creatinine, urea/BUN, SDMA, phosphorus, electrolytes, and red blood cell levels.
  • Urinalysis: urine concentration, protein levels, and sediment.
  • Blood pressure: as both high and low blood pressure can be harmful.
  • Weight and body condition: to identify weight loss or muscle wasting early.

What You Can Do at Home

  • Offer kidney-friendly food and monitor appetite, noting what your dog eats each day.
  • Encourage good hydration with fresh water, wet food, and, if advised, subcutaneous fluids.
  • Track urination (amount, frequency, and ease of passing urine) and note any accidents or straining.
  • Watch for nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, or behaviour changes and report them promptly to your vet.
  • Give medications consistently and as prescribed, and keep a simple log of doses and any side effects.

The overall aim is to balance medical treatment with practical, loving home care that keeps your dog’s day-to-day life as normal and enjoyable as possible.

FAQs

Q: What are the main causes of kidney failure in dogs?

A: Kidney failure can result from many different problems. Acute kidney injury is often caused by toxins (like antifreeze or certain human medications), severe dehydration or shock, infections such as leptospirosis, or urinary blockages. Chronic kidney disease is more commonly linked to age-related wear and tear, inherited or structural kidney problems, immune-mediated diseases, long-term effects of toxins or infections, or previous acute injury that left lasting damage.

Q: How can I recognize the signs of kidney failure in my dog?

A: Early signs include increased thirst and urination, reduced appetite, weight loss, and lethargy. As the condition worsens, you may see vomiting, bad breath, mouth ulcers, weakness, or changes in urination (very little urine, accidents, or straining). These signs are not specific to kidney disease, so your dog should be examined by a vet and have blood and urine tests to confirm what is going on.

Q: What treatment options are available for dogs with kidney failure?

A: Treatment options range from hospital-based intravenous fluids, electrolyte correction, and management of the underlying cause (for example, toxins, infections, or blockages) to long-term home care with kidney-friendly diets, blood pressure and proteinuria medications, phosphate binders, anti-nausea drugs, and sometimes subcutaneous fluids. In severe acute cases, referral centres may offer dialysis. Your vet will tailor a plan to your dog’s specific needs and stage of disease.

Q: Is there a specific diet recommended for dogs with kidney issues?

A: Yes. Most dogs with kidney failure benefit from a veterinary prescription renal diet that is lower in phosphorus, provides moderate but high-quality protein, and has controlled sodium plus added omega-3 fatty acids. These diets are carefully formulated based on current kidney disease guidelines. Any diet change should be introduced gradually and under veterinary supervision.

Q: How can I support my dog at home if they have kidney failure?

A: You can help by feeding the recommended diet, encouraging water intake, giving medications on schedule, and monitoring appetite, weight, urination, and behaviour. Some owners are taught to give fluids under the skin at home. Keeping a simple daily log of what your dog eats, drinks, and how they seem can be very helpful for your vet when adjusting treatment.

Q: Are there any medications or supplements that can help dogs with kidney failure?

A: Commonly used medications include blood pressure drugs, ACE inhibitors or other agents to reduce protein loss in urine, phosphate binders, anti-nausea medications, antacids, and appetite stimulants. Omega-3 supplements may be recommended in some cases. All medications and supplements should be prescribed or approved by your vet, as some products can actually worsen kidney problems if used incorrectly.

Q: What is the prognosis for dogs diagnosed with kidney failure?

A: Prognosis varies widely and depends on whether the problem is acute or chronic, how severe it is at diagnosis, the underlying cause, and how the dog responds to treatment. Some dogs with acute kidney injury can recover much of their kidney function, while others are left with chronic disease. Dogs with chronic kidney failure may live from months to years with appropriate care. Your vet can give the most realistic guidance based on your dog’s test results and day-to-day condition.

Q: How often should I take my dog for check-ups if they have kidney failure?

A: Dogs with stable, early-stage chronic kidney disease might be rechecked every 3–6 months, while those with more advanced disease or recent changes in appetite, weight, or blood values may need visits every few weeks to months. After any major change in medication or diet, blood and urine tests are usually repeated to ensure the new plan is safe and effective.

Q: Can kidney failure in dogs be managed long-term?

A: In many cases, yes. While kidney failure is typically not curable, careful long-term management with diet, medications, and regular monitoring can keep dogs comfortable and active for extended periods. The focus is on slowing progression, preventing crises such as dehydration or severe toxin buildup, and maintaining a good quality of life.

Q: What lifestyle changes can I make to help my dog with kidney failure?

A: Helpful changes include feeding a kidney-friendly diet, ensuring constant access to fresh water, avoiding high-salt or high-phosphorus treats, giving medications consistently, and keeping stress low with gentle exercise and a predictable routine. Keep all human medications, antifreeze, and household chemicals out of reach, and seek prompt veterinary care for vomiting, diarrhoea, or sudden behaviour changes. These small, consistent actions can make a big difference to your dog’s comfort and wellbeing.
