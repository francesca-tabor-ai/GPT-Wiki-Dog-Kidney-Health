Understanding Kidney Disease in Dogs

Kidney disease in dogs (often called chronic kidney disease, or CKD) happens when the kidneys gradually lose their ability to filter waste products from the blood. As this filtering ability declines, toxins and excess fluids can build up in the body, affecting your dog’s appetite, energy levels, and overall wellbeing.

Common causes include age-related wear and tear, genetic conditions, infections (such as tick-borne or parasitic diseases like Leishmania), immune-mediated damage to the kidney filters (glomeruli), and exposure to certain toxins or medications. In many older dogs, there may be more than one contributing factor.

Early and Late Signs to Watch For

  • Drinking more water than usual
  • Urinating more frequently or producing larger volumes of urine
  • Weight loss and a “bony” appearance over time
  • Reduced appetite or being very fussy with food
  • Lethargy, sleeping more, or less interest in walks and play
  • Bad breath (sometimes with a “metallic” or ammonia-like smell)
  • Vomiting, nausea, or drooling

Kidney disease is usually progressive and not fully reversible, but with the right combination of medical treatment and nutrition, many dogs can enjoy a good quality of life for months or even years.

Nutritional Needs for Dogs with Kidney Disease

Diet is one of the most important tools for managing kidney disease in dogs. The goal is to reduce the kidneys’ workload, slow progression where possible, and keep your dog comfortable and well nourished.

Key Dietary Goals

  • Moderate to low protein: Enough high-quality protein to maintain muscle mass, but not so much that it produces excessive nitrogen waste your dog’s kidneys must clear.
  • Controlled phosphorus: Lowering dietary phosphorus helps protect remaining kidney tissue and can slow disease progression.
  • Controlled sodium: Helps manage blood pressure, which is often elevated in dogs with CKD.
  • Higher moisture: Wet food or added water/broth supports hydration and helps the kidneys flush toxins.
  • Adequate calories: Dogs with CKD may lose weight easily, so calorie-dense but kidney-friendly foods are important.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Marine-based omega-3s (from fish or algae oil) may support kidney blood flow and help reduce inflammation.
  • Balanced vitamins and minerals: CKD can change how the body handles certain nutrients, so renal diets are carefully balanced for this.

Prescription renal diets are designed around these principles, but some dogs need a combination of commercial renal food and carefully planned home-cooked meals, especially if they are very fussy eaters.

Homemade Diet Options for Dogs with Kidney Disease

Home-cooked diets can be helpful for dogs who dislike commercial renal food or need extra variety. However, they must be carefully balanced. Always involve your veterinarian and, ideally, a board-certified veterinary nutritionist when creating a long-term home diet.

General Principles for Home-Cooked Renal Diets

  • Use high-quality, lean protein in modest amounts (e.g., skinless chicken breast, turkey breast, egg whites, or white fish).
  • Base the meal around low-phosphorus carbohydrates such as white rice, pasta, or well-cooked sweet potato.
  • Add low-phosphorus vegetables like green beans, zucchini, or peeled carrots in small amounts.
  • Include a veterinary-approved omega-3 supplement (fish or algae oil) if recommended.
  • Use a calcium source or phosphate binder if your vet advises it, to help control phosphorus absorption.
  • Avoid adding salt, seasoning, onions, garlic, or rich fatty foods.

Example Home-Cooked Renal-Support Meal (for a small dog)

Exact quantities should be tailored to your dog’s weight and lab results by a veterinary professional. The following is a simplified example only:

  • 70 g cooked, skinless chicken breast (boiled or poached, no seasoning)
  • 40 g cooked white rice
  • 30 g boiled sweet potato
  • 10 g cooked green beans or zucchini
  • 1 teaspoon fish oil (if recommended by your vet)
  • Vet-recommended mineral or kidney-support supplement as prescribed

Foods typically to avoid in home diets for kidney disease include organ meats (liver, kidney), bones, cheese, sardines or fish with skin and bones, high-phosphorus treats, and salty processed meats such as ham or bacon.

How to Transition Your Dog to a Kidney-Friendly Diet

Most dogs adjust better to diet changes when they are made gradually. Abrupt changes can upset the stomach or make a fussy dog refuse the new food altogether.

Suggested Transition Schedule (7–10 Days)

  • Days 1–3: 75% old food, 25% new kidney diet.
  • Days 4–6: 50% old food, 50% new diet.
  • Days 7–10: 25% old food, 75% new diet.
  • Day 11 onward: 100% new kidney diet.

If your dog is very reluctant, try warming the food slightly, adding a little warm water or low-sodium broth, or mixing in a small amount of a favourite kidney-safe topper like boiled chicken breast. Appetite stimulants, anti-nausea medications, or antacid therapy may also be used under veterinary supervision to support the transition.

Monitoring Your Dog's Health and Diet Adjustments

Once your dog is on a kidney-friendly diet, ongoing monitoring is vital. Kidney disease can change over time, and diet often needs small adjustments along the way.

What to Track at Home

  • Appetite: How keenly your dog eats each meal (you can use a simple 1–5 score).
  • Water intake: Any sudden increase or decrease in drinking.
  • Urination: Frequency, volume, and color of urine.
  • Weight and body shape: Whether your dog is losing or gaining weight.
  • Energy and behaviour: Interest in walks, play, and interaction.
  • Signs of nausea: Lip licking, drooling, turning away from food, or vomiting.

Your veterinarian will usually recommend regular blood and urine tests to monitor kidney values (such as creatinine, urea/BUN, SDMA, and phosphorus), urine protein levels, and blood pressure. If appetite drops off, vomiting appears, or your dog seems more lethargic than usual, it may be time for a prompt veterinary recheck and possible diet or medication adjustment.

FAQs

Q: What are the symptoms of kidney disease in dogs?

A: Common symptoms include increased thirst and urination, reduced appetite, weight loss, lethargy, and bad breath. In more advanced cases, you may also see vomiting, diarrhea, weakness, or ulcers in the mouth. These signs are not specific to kidney disease, so blood and urine tests are needed for diagnosis.

Q: How can diet impact a dog's kidney health?

A: Diet can significantly influence how hard the kidneys have to work. Kidney-friendly diets use moderate to low amounts of high-quality protein, restricted phosphorus, and controlled sodium, plus increased moisture and omega-3 fats. This combination helps reduce toxin buildup, support blood pressure control, and can slow the progression of kidney damage while maintaining your dog’s weight and comfort.

Q: What should be the protein content in dog food for kidney disease?

A: For most dogs with chronic kidney disease, the goal is moderate but high-quality protein rather than very high or very low levels. Many veterinary kidney diets provide roughly 14–20% protein on a dry matter basis, but the ideal amount depends on your dog’s stage of disease, muscle condition, and any other health issues. Your vet or a veterinary nutritionist can help choose the most appropriate level.

Q: Are there specific ingredients to avoid in dog food for kidney disease?

A: Yes. You should generally avoid high-phosphorus and high-sodium ingredients. This includes organ meats (liver, kidney), bones and bone meal, cheese and many other dairy products, fish with bones or skin (like sardines), processed meats (ham, bacon, sausages), and very salty treats such as jerky made for humans. High-protein, high-purine foods should also be limited in dogs on certain medications, such as allopurinol.

Q: Can dogs with kidney disease eat treats or snacks?

A: They can, but treats should be kidney-friendly and given in moderation. Good options include small pieces of cooked chicken breast, cooked egg white, plain rice or pasta, apple slices (without seeds), blueberries, or green beans. Avoid salty, fatty, or high-phosphorus treats. As a rule of thumb, treats should make up no more than about 10% of your dog’s daily calorie intake.

Q: How do I know if my dog’s food is suitable for kidney disease?

A: Look for diets labelled as “renal,” “kidney support,” or “kidney care” that are sold through veterinarians, and check that your vet agrees it is appropriate for your dog’s stage of disease. For any non-prescription or home-cooked diet, your vet should review the nutrient profile (especially protein, phosphorus, and sodium levels) and your dog’s latest blood and urine test results.

Q: Should I consult a veterinarian before changing my dog's diet?

A: Yes. Any dog with confirmed or suspected kidney disease should have diet changes planned in consultation with a veterinarian. Sudden changes in protein, phosphorus, or sodium intake can affect bloodwork, blood pressure, and how well medications work. Your vet can also help you transition safely and decide if supportive medications, such as anti-nausea drugs or appetite stimulants, are needed.

Q: How often should I feed my dog with kidney disease?

A: Many dogs with kidney disease do best with smaller, more frequent meals—usually two to three meals per day instead of one large meal. This can help reduce nausea, keep energy levels steadier, and make it easier for fussy eaters to consume enough calories over the day.

Q: What are the best sources of protein for dogs with kidney disease?

A: The best proteins are highly digestible and relatively low in phosphorus. Examples include egg whites, skinless chicken breast, turkey breast, and some types of white fish. In commercial renal diets, the protein sources are chosen and carefully balanced specifically for dogs with reduced kidney function.

Q: Can I make my own dog food for a pet with kidney disease?

A: Yes, but it should be done with veterinary guidance. Long-term home-cooked diets must be balanced with the right vitamins and minerals and tailored to your dog’s bloodwork and stage of disease. A veterinary nutritionist can formulate a recipe that meets your dog’s needs and works alongside their medications and monitoring plan.
